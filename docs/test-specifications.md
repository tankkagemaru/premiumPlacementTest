## 0. About this document

This document is the definitive specification for the Premium Language Centre CEFR Placement Test (PLC-CPT). It governs what the test measures, how items are written, how scores are produced, and how CEFR levels are assigned. Any change to the test must be reflected here before it goes operational.

**Audience.** Item writers, test developers, language teachers, administrators making placement decisions, and anyone evaluating the test's claims.

**Status legend used throughout.**
- **AGREED** — confirmed policy, do not change without governance approval.
- **PROPOSED** — recommended position pending sign-off by Madam Waty / Academic Office.
- **PROVISIONAL** — operational placeholder used until empirical evidence (pilot, calibration) replaces it.

**Open policy decisions requiring sign-off** are listed in §17 *Governance & Pending Decisions*. Where this document marks a position as `PROPOSED`, the reader should understand that the operational test will follow that position unless the Academic Office directs otherwise.

## 1. Purpose, population, and stakes

### 1.1 Purpose

The PLC-CPT exists to **place incoming students into the appropriate level of instruction at Premium Language Centre**. It is an internal placement tool, not a certification, not an admissions screening, and not a substitute for any external CEFR-aligned examination.

### 1.2 Decisions the test informs

- Which course level a student enters at PLC (A1, A2, B1, B2, or C1+ track).
- Whether a student needs a follow-up oral interview (triggered for any C1+ result).
- Diagnostic information for the assigned teacher (per-skill profile).

### 1.3 Decisions the test does NOT inform

- External certification of language proficiency (use Aptis, IELTS, Cambridge, etc.).
- Employment or university admissions decisions.
- Speaking or writing proficiency. The PLC-CPT measures receptive skills only.

### 1.4 Target population

- **Primary**: incoming students at PLC, ages 14 and above, mixed L1 backgrounds with Malay, Mandarin, and Tamil being the most common.
- **Secondary**: existing PLC students taking a re-placement after a hiatus, or for confirmation of progression.

### 1.5 Stakes

**Low-stakes internal placement.** Students can request a re-test after a teacher-granted retake (see PLC-CPT Operational Procedures). No life-affecting decision rests on a single test result.

### 1.6 Test mode

Computer-delivered, computer-adaptive within sections. Web-based, accessible from any modern browser. Supervised administration is preferred but unsupervised administration is permitted with the understanding that unsupervised results may be flagged for confirmation interview.

## 2. Construct definitions

The test measures three constructs, each reported separately. The constructs are derived from the *Common European Framework of Reference for Languages* (Council of Europe, 2001; companion volume 2020) and operationalised using the *English Profile* family of resources.

### 2.1 Grammar & Vocabulary (G&V)

**Construct.** Receptive control of lexicogrammatical knowledge — the structures and lexis a language user must recognise to comprehend written and spoken English at each CEFR level.

**Theoretical basis.** Grammar and vocabulary are not standalone skills under the CEFR framework but constitute *competences* that underpin all four traditional skills (Council of Europe 2001, Chapter 5). Empirical evidence (Kim & Crossley 2020) using confirmatory factor analysis on 9,700 ECCE test-takers shows that lexicogrammar loads on a shared latent construct with reading and listening comprehension, justifying both its inclusion as a distinct measure and its use as a tie-breaker when other skill scores are borderline.

**Item types.**
- Multiple-choice cloze (gapped sentence, four options, one key).
- Lexical multiple choice (vocabulary definition or substitution, four options).
- Form-meaning matching (limited use, for collocation and lexical chunks).

**CEFR mapping basis.**
- Grammar items mapped using the *English Grammar Profile* (O'Keeffe & Mark 2017; available at englishprofile.org), which assigns ~1,200 grammatical competence statements to specific CEFR levels.
- Vocabulary items mapped using the *English Vocabulary Profile* (Capel 2012; available at englishprofile.org), which assigns ~15,000 word senses to CEFR levels.

### 2.2 Listening

**Construct.** Comprehension of spoken English across a range of registers, accents, speaker numbers, text lengths, and discourse types, mapped to the CEFR *Overall Listening Comprehension* descriptors and sub-scales (Council of Europe 2020, Chapter 3.1).

**Sub-construct coverage.**

| CEFR descriptor | Operationalised in test as |
|---|---|
| Understanding conversation between others (B1+) | Multi-speaker dialogue items |
| Listening as a member of a live audience (B2+) | Lecture / presentation excerpts |
| Listening to announcements and instructions | Short transactional items |
| Listening to audio media and recordings | Standard recorded passages |

**Audio characteristics by CEFR band.**

| Band | Length per clip | Speaker count | Speech rate | Accent |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A1–A2 | 20–40 sec | 1 speaker | Slow, clear | Neutral (standard British or American) |
| B1 | 45–75 sec | 1–2 speakers | Natural, clear | Neutral, occasional Malaysian/regional |
| B2 | 75–110 sec | 2–3 speakers | Natural | Mixed: British, American, Australian, Malaysian |
| C1+ | 100–150 sec | 2+ speakers | Natural to fast | Mixed, including some non-standard varieties |

**Multi-speaker mandate.** Per Madam Waty's direction (and the B2+ CEFR descriptors which require comprehension of "animated conversation between native speakers"), at least **30% of items at A2/B1 and 50% of items at B2+** must be drawn from multi-speaker audio.

**Items per audio clip.** At A1–A2, one item per clip is acceptable. At B1 and above, **2–3 items per clip** is the standard. This both improves per-recording reliability and reduces production cost.

### 2.3 Reading

**Construct.** Comprehension of written English across genres, lengths, and discourse complexities, mapped to the CEFR *Overall Reading Comprehension* descriptors and sub-scales (Council of Europe 2020, Chapter 3.2).

**Sub-construct coverage.**

| CEFR descriptor | Operationalised in test as |
|---|---|
| Reading correspondence | Short emails, notes, social media |
| Reading for orientation | Signs, schedules, menus (A1–A2 only) |
| Reading for information and argument | Articles, opinion pieces, reports |
| Reading instructions | Procedural texts |
| Reading as a leisure activity | Narrative excerpts |

**Text characteristics by CEFR band.**

| Band | Length | Vocabulary | Syntactic complexity |
|---|---|---|---|
| A1–A2 | 30–80 words | Common, high-frequency | Simple sentences, common connectors |
| B1 | 100–180 words | Mostly common, some less frequent | Compound and simple complex |
| B2 | 200–300 words | Range including topic-specific | Complex with embedded clauses |
| C1+ | 300–450 words | Extended range, some academic | Complex, including marked structures |

**Items per passage.** **3–5 items per passage** at B1+. A1–A2 may use single-question short texts.

## 3. Test structure

### 3.1 Section order

**AGREED** — Sections are administered in fixed order. Students cannot skip or reorder.

1. **Grammar & Vocabulary** (warm-up + tie-breaker function)
2. **Listening** (placed second so audio focus does not fatigue text-heavy work)
3. **Reading** (placed last; most cognitively demanding extended attention)

### 3.2 Item counts and timing

**PROPOSED**

| Section | Items | Adaptive | Estimated time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grammar & Vocabulary | 15 | Yes | 12 minutes |
| Listening | 20 (across 7–8 audio clips) | Yes (by clip) | 20 minutes |
| Reading | 20 (across 5–6 passages) | Yes (by passage) | 18 minutes |
| **Total** | **55 items** | | **~50 minutes** |

Plus 5 minutes of pre-test instructions and consent acknowledgement. Total seat time: ~55 minutes.

**Rationale for 15 G&V items.** Reliability (KR-20) of a 10-item subtest can fall below 0.70 unless every item has high discrimination. 15 items provides headroom and is consistent with Aptis Core (~25 G&V items in approx. 25 min). The expert email recommended "10–15 items, ~10 minutes"; this specification adopts the upper bound to protect reliability.

### 3.3 Adaptivity

Within each section, item selection follows an adaptive algorithm.

- **Pre-calibration phase** (until pilot complete): heuristic difficulty steering based on running running ability estimate, drawing items within ±1.5 difficulty bands of the current estimate, with skill-area quotas to ensure construct coverage. This is the current `App.jsx` algorithm, scoped to a section's item bank.
- **Post-calibration phase** (after pilot): Rasch (1-parameter IRT) theta estimation, maximum-information item selection. Standard error of theta serves as the stopping rule, with a fixed lower bound on item count.

### 3.4 Item formats

**Multiple choice, single key, four options** is the operational item format for all sections at launch. This is chosen for simplicity, scorability, and the existing item-bank format. Other formats (matching, gap-fill with banks, ordering) may be added in future revisions if construct validity gaps appear.

### 3.5 Accessibility

- Audio items: replayable up to **2 times** per item. After that the audio is locked.
- Text: minimum 16px sans-serif body text, with the option to enlarge via standard browser controls.
- No timed item lockouts within a section (only the section-level time limit applies).
- Audio captions are **not provided** during the test (would compromise the listening construct). A teacher-supplied accommodation procedure exists for students with documented hearing impairment.

### 3.6 Security

- Items presented one at a time, with no review/back navigation within a section.
- Section order locked.
- Copy/paste disabled in the test surface.
- Right-click disabled on item content.
- Item order randomised within difficulty bands so two simultaneous test-takers do not see items in identical order.
- Items rotate out of the active pool periodically (see §11 *Item bank management*).

## 4. Content specifications

### 4.1 Topic universe

Items must be drawn from the following topic categories, broadly aligned with CEFR domains (personal, public, occupational, educational):

- Personal: daily routine, family, hobbies, food, health
- Social: friendships, community, events, travel
- Educational: studying, classes, exams, libraries
- Occupational: workplace situations, communication, services
- Public: news (neutral topics), environment, transportation, technology

### 4.2 Topics excluded

- Politically partisan content
- Religion-specific content beyond general cultural reference
- Highly culturally-specific content that disadvantages students unfamiliar with one regional context
- Trauma, graphic violence, explicit content
- Content requiring specialist knowledge unrelated to language (e.g., specific scientific or legal procedures)

### 4.3 Voice talent and accent variety (Listening)

**PROPOSED**

Listening recordings should include, at minimum:
- 2 British English voices (one male, one female)
- 2 American English voices (one male, one female)
- 1 Australian English voice
- 1 Malaysian English voice (for ecological validity given the primary population)

A2–C1+ items should be distributed across these voices such that no single voice represents more than 35% of items at any CEFR band above A1.

### 4.4 Item characteristics

**Stems.** Clear, unambiguous, no negation in stems unless deliberately testing negative comprehension (and then highlighted typographically).

**Distractors.** Three plausible distractors per item. Each distractor must be:
- Grammatically parallel to the key
- Plausible to a student below the target level
- Defensibly wrong (a panel of three reviewers must agree the key is the *only* correct answer)

**Avoid.** "All of the above", "none of the above", trivia, items where elimination strategy substitutes for construct knowledge.

## 5. Item-writing guidelines

### 5.1 Authoring workflow

1. Item writer authors item with metadata: skill, target CEFR level, target difficulty (1–10 within band), topic, source if applicable.
2. Item is recorded in the staging item bank with status `draft`.
3. Item undergoes peer review by a second CEFR-qualified reviewer (§5.2).
4. If accepted, item moves to status `pilot-ready`.
5. Once piloted (≥30 responses), item analysis runs (§9.3). Items meeting acceptance criteria move to status `operational`. Items failing move to `revision` or `retired`.

### 5.2 Peer review checklist

Every item is reviewed against the checklist in *Appendix C*. Both the writer and the reviewer must sign off before the item enters pilot status.

### 5.3 CEFR level assignment

The writer's initial CEFR assignment is **provisional**. Final CEFR assignment for each item is set during the standard-setting workshop (§7.4). After standard-setting, an item's CEFR level may differ from the writer's intent — this is normal and indicates the writer or the rubric was off-target.

### 5.4 Item style guide (abbreviated)

- One construct per item. An item should test one thing, clearly.
- No double-barrelled stems ("Which of the following is true and supports the author's argument?").
- Numerical or factual answers must not depend on world knowledge outside the text/audio.
- For listening, the key must be defensibly inferable from audio alone — not from item stem + general knowledge.

## 6. Scoring framework

### 6.1 Raw scores

For each section, raw score = number of items answered correctly. No partial credit. No penalty for guessing.

### 6.2 Scaled scores

**PROPOSED** — A 0–50 scaled score per section, derived from the ability estimate.

- Pre-calibration: ability estimate from heuristic, then linearly transformed to a 0–50 scale anchored at empirical maximum and minimum observed in pilot.
- Post-calibration: theta from Rasch model, transformed to a 0–50 scale anchored at theta = -3 (scaled 0) and theta = +3 (scaled 50).

### 6.3 Per-skill CEFR level

Each section's scaled score maps to a CEFR level using **cut-scores** established by standard-setting (§7).

Cut-score table (PROVISIONAL — to be confirmed by standard-setting workshop):

| Scaled score | CEFR level (per skill) |
|---|---|
| 0–9 | A1 |
| 10–19 | A2 |
| 20–29 | B1 |
| 30–39 | B2 |
| 40–50 | C1+ |

### 6.4 Overall CEFR derivation

**PROPOSED** — Median-with-G&V-tiebreaker.

1. Compute the three per-skill CEFR levels (Reading, Listening, G&V).
2. Take the median of the three.
3. **Tie-breaker rule**: if the three levels are split such that the median rule is ambiguous (e.g., the three levels are A2 / B1 / B2 → median = B1, no ambiguity; but if two skills are at one level and the third is one band lower OR higher, see below):

| Pattern | Overall |
|---|---|
| All three levels equal | That level |
| Two equal, third one band lower or higher | The two-of-three level |
| Three different levels (e.g., A2 / B1 / B2) | Median |
| Two equal AND third on a borderline (within 2 scaled points of next band) | Two-of-three level, **but** G&V score may push to the next band if borderline favours it |

The borderline rule operationalises Aptis's principle that "if a score falls on the threshold between two CEFR levels in a particular skill, the grammar and vocabulary score is taken into account to assign the correct CEFR level" (British Council, Aptis Results & Scoring page).

### 6.5 Soft ceiling at C1+

**AGREED** — The test reports C1+ as the maximum CEFR level. The PLC-CPT does not distinguish C1 from C2.

Rationale: distinguishing C1 from C2 reliably requires items genuinely calibrated at the C2 level, which in turn requires C2-qualified item writers and a substantially larger item bank than the PLC-CPT's scope supports. Any student reaching C1+ on the PLC-CPT is **referred for an oral interview** with the Academic Office, which makes the final placement decision for that band.

This matches the practice of most language centres globally and is honest about the test's measurement limit.

### 6.6 Provisional results

Until the pilot is complete and standard-setting has run, all CEFR labels output by the PLC-CPT are flagged as **provisional** in student-facing communication and teacher dashboards. The label "Provisional CEFR — to be confirmed by your teacher" appears next to results during this period.

## 7. CEFR alignment and standard-setting

### 7.1 Item-level CEFR alignment

Each item carries a CEFR label assigned by the writer (provisional) and confirmed during standard-setting (operational). Confirmed alignment is the operational label used by the test engine.

### 7.2 Standard-setting procedure

**Method: Modified Angoff.** This is the dominant standard-setting method for CEFR-aligned tests, used by Cambridge, ETS, and Pearson for their language assessments.

**Procedure**:

1. **Rater panel.** 3–5 raters per session. Each rater must hold:
   - A minimum of CELTA, DELTA, or equivalent teaching qualification
   - Three years of CEFR-aligned teaching experience
   - Familiarity with the *English Profile* resources
2. **Rater training.** Half-day workshop:
   - Review CEFR descriptors at A1, A2, B1, B2, C1 with examples
   - Practice ratings on benchmark items (items with established CEFR levels, e.g., from English Profile samples)
   - Calibration discussion to align rater understanding of "minimally competent at level X"
3. **Rating round 1.** For each cut-score (A1/A2 boundary, A2/B1, B1/B2, B2/C1), each rater estimates: "What proportion of *just barely at this level* test-takers would get this item correct?" Each rater rates each item independently.
4. **Discussion.** Raters discuss items where ratings diverged by >20 percentage points. No pressure to agree, but each rater articulates their reasoning.
5. **Rating round 2.** Raters re-rate after discussion (allowed to change or keep original ratings).
6. **Cut-score calculation.** For each CEFR boundary, cut-score = average rater rating across all items × number of items at that level. This produces a "probable raw score for minimally competent test-taker at this level".

### 7.3 Standard-setting cadence

- Initial standard-setting: after pilot data is collected (Section 8).
- Refresh: every 3 years, or when the item bank turnover exceeds 50%, whichever is sooner.
- Triggered refresh: if annual technical report shows classification accuracy < 75% at any boundary.

### 7.4 Cut-score validation

After standard-setting, validate cut-scores by:
- Classification consistency check (Cohen's kappa on test-retest sample, target ≥ 0.7)
- Concordance with teacher placement on a 50-student sample (target ≥ 80% agreement within ±1 CEFR band)
- Comparison against external benchmark where available (e.g., students with prior IELTS/Aptis scores)

## 8. Pilot and calibration plan

### 8.1 Pilot phase A — Item analysis

**Sample**: Minimum 100 test-takers. Stratified across expected proficiency levels: ~20 each at A1, A2, B1, B2, C1+ (recruitment from existing PLC students and incoming registrations).

**Data collected per item**:
- p-value (proportion correct)
- Point-biserial correlation (item-total)
- Distractor analysis (proportion choosing each option)
- Average response time

**Acceptance criteria**:
- p-value: 0.20 ≤ p ≤ 0.90 (items outside this band are too easy or too hard for the pilot population; may be reserved for specific CEFR bands)
- Point-biserial: rpb ≥ 0.20 (items below this discriminate poorly and are candidates for revision or retirement)
- No distractor chosen by 0% of test-takers (a dead distractor; revise)
- Average response time within 1.5× the median for items of the same length (extreme outliers suggest ambiguity)

### 8.2 Pilot phase B — Rasch calibration

After items pass classical analysis (§8.1), calibrate using Rasch (1PL) IRT.

**Why Rasch?** Rasch gives item difficulty parameters on a logit scale, allowing principled adaptive item selection (maximum-information matching of theta to item difficulty). Sample requirements are modest — 100 students provides stable estimates for most items. Used by ETS, Cambridge, and ALTE.

**Outputs**:
- Item difficulty parameter (`b`) on logit scale
- Person ability estimate (`theta`) for each pilot test-taker
- Item fit statistics (infit and outfit mean-square, target 0.7–1.3)

Items with poor fit (outside 0.7–1.3) are flagged for review.

### 8.3 Pilot phase C — Standard-setting

After items are calibrated, run the modified Angoff procedure (§7.2) on the operational item bank. Output: cut-scores per skill.

### 8.4 Differential Item Functioning (DIF) screening

Given mixed L1 backgrounds in the population, run DIF analysis after pilot Phase B:

- Mantel-Haenszel procedure for each item, comparing largest L1 groups pairwise (e.g., Malay-L1 vs. Mandarin-L1).
- Flag items with |Δ| ≥ 1.0 (ETS "C" classification — large DIF) for review.
- Items showing large DIF without a clear construct-relevant explanation are revised or retired.

### 8.5 Pilot timeline (indicative)

| Month | Activity |
|---|---|
| 1 | Item bank reaches minimum size (§11.1). Pilot recruitment opens. |
| 2–3 | Pilot administration. |
| 4 | Item analysis. Initial discards. |
| 5 | Rasch calibration. DIF screening. |
| 6 | Standard-setting workshop. |
| 7 | Cut-score validation. Operational launch (provisional → operational). |

### 8.6 Continuous calibration

After operational launch, every quarter:
- Add new items to the bank (target 10–15% of operational set per year).
- Re-run item analysis on items with ≥50 new responses.
- Update Rasch parameters using common-item linking (anchor items shared between calibration sessions).

## 9. Psychometric standards

### 9.1 Reliability

**Target**: Cronbach's alpha (or KR-20) ≥ 0.70 per section, ≥ 0.80 for overall test.

**Procedure**: Calculated each quarter on all completed attempts in the period. Reported in the Annual Technical Report.

**If targets are not met**: investigate. Likely causes are too few items, poor item discrimination, or population heterogeneity. Action: expand item count or replace poorly-discriminating items.

### 9.2 Validity

The PLC-CPT validity argument rests on:

**Content validity.** Items map to CEFR descriptors via the *English Grammar Profile* and *English Vocabulary Profile* (§2.1). Each section is structured around the CEFR sub-scales (§2.2, §2.3).

**Construct validity.** Confirmed via factor analysis after pilot — three-factor structure (G&V, Listening, Reading) should hold, with G&V loading on the shared latent factor (Kim & Crossley 2020).

**Concurrent validity.** Where students have external benchmarks (IELTS, Aptis, Cambridge), PLC-CPT scores are compared. Target Pearson r ≥ 0.6 with the equivalent skill on the external benchmark.

**Predictive validity.** Annually, sample 30 placed students and survey their teachers 8 weeks into the course on whether the placement was correct. Target ≥ 80% confirmed placements within ±1 CEFR band.

### 9.3 Measurement precision

Target standard error of measurement (SEM) ≤ 0.5 CEFR level (i.e., placement accurate to within half a band 68% of the time). Reported as conditional SEM at each cut-score boundary.

### 9.4 Fairness

The test is screened for DIF (§8.4) and adjusted as needed. The Annual Technical Report includes a fairness section reporting:
- DIF flags per L1 group
- Mean score differences across L1 groups
- Items revised or retired for fairness reasons

## 10. Reporting framework

### 10.1 Student-facing report

Delivered by email (post-approval) and visible in the student portal.

**Contents**:
- Overall CEFR level (or "Provisional CEFR" before standard-setting complete)
- Per-skill CEFR level: Reading, Listening, Grammar & Vocabulary
- Per-skill scaled score (0–50)
- A short narrative paragraph describing what the level means and what the student can typically do at that level (sourced from CEFR Companion Volume can-do statements)
- For C1+ results: notice that the result is referred for oral interview confirmation

**Tone**: encouraging, descriptive, never deficit-framed. Students see what they *can* do at their level, not what they "failed".

### 10.2 Teacher-facing report

Visible in the teacher dashboard.

**Contents**:
- All of §10.1
- Raw scores per section
- Standard error of the score (post-calibration)
- Skill-area p-values within the test (where did the student get most wrong — vocabulary, listening to dialogues, reading inference, etc.)
- Diagnostic recommendations (e.g., "This student is solidly B1 in reading but A2 in listening. Recommend listening-strengthening focus in early weeks.")

### 10.3 Internal report (Academic Office)

Aggregated, used quarterly:
- Score distributions by section
- Reliability and SEM trends
- DIF flags
- Pilot/calibration updates
- Item bank size and turnover

## 11. Item bank management

### 11.1 Target bank sizes

| Section | Operational items | Total bank (≥3× operational) |
|---|---|---|
| Grammar & Vocabulary | 15 per administration | ~150 items, ~30 per CEFR level (A1–C1+) |
| Listening | 20 per administration (~7 clips) | ~25 clips total, ~60 items, ~12 per CEFR level |
| Reading | 20 per administration (~6 passages) | ~20 passages total, ~80 items, ~16 per CEFR level |

**Why 3x?** Items leak through informal sharing. A 3x bank lets the adaptive engine vary item presentation across test-takers and supports annual retirement.

### 11.2 Exposure control

- No single item appears in more than 30% of administrations in a 30-day rolling window.
- Items with elevated exposure are temporarily withheld and rotated back later.
- Items flagged in social media monitoring are retired immediately.

### 11.3 Item retirement criteria

An item is retired (moved to status `retired`, not used operationally) if any of the following:
- p-value drifts outside acceptable range over time (suggesting compromise)
- DIF flag emerges in fairness review
- Content becomes dated (technology references, news events, etc.)
- Item is reported as having appeared on third-party sites
- 5 years operational tenure (default retirement age unless explicitly renewed)

### 11.4 Item replacement cycle

Each year, retire and replace approximately 10–15% of the operational item set. New items go through the full authoring → review → pilot pipeline before operational use.

## 12. Test administration

### 12.1 Delivery

Web browser. Modern Chromium-based browsers, Firefox, Safari supported. No mobile delivery for the initial version (audio quality and screen size compromise the construct).

### 12.2 Time limits

- Each section is timed (§3.2).
- A 60-second warning appears at the end of each section.
- Unfinished items at section end are marked incorrect.

### 12.3 Pause and resume

- Within a section: no pause permitted.
- Between sections: a short break (≤2 minutes) is permitted.
- Mid-test crash: the system records progress to the last completed section. The student may resume the failed section once. If the section was nearly complete (>80%), the partial result is recorded and flagged for review.

### 12.4 Supervised vs. unsupervised

- **Supervised**: preferred for first-time placement and for any student where stakes warrant. Conducted at PLC under invigilation.
- **Unsupervised**: permitted for remote takers and for confirmation re-tests. Results from unsupervised administrations are flagged in the teacher dashboard with a recommendation to confirm via brief oral check.

### 12.5 Accommodations

- Documented hearing impairment: extended time on listening (no change to construct), or substitution with a different listening administration mode (case-by-case).
- Documented learning differences: extended time on all sections (1.5× or 2× per documentation).
- All accommodations granted by the Academic Office before the test, recorded in the student record.

### 13.1 Item review checklist

See Appendix C. Used for every item before pilot status.

### 13.2 Annual Technical Report

Internal document, prepared by the test custodian, reviewed by the Academic Office. Includes:
- Operational summary (number of administrations, demographics)
- Reliability per section and overall
- Validity evidence updates (concurrent, predictive)
- Cut-score classification accuracy
- DIF/fairness summary
- Item bank size, turnover, and retirement reasons
- Any incidents (compromised items, technical failures, complaints)
- Recommended changes for the coming year

First report: 12 months after operational launch.

### 13.3 Change control

Changes to this specification follow the change control process in §17. Substantive changes (cut-scores, section structure, scoring rule) require re-issuing the document version. Editorial changes (wording, typos) can be issued as minor revisions.

### 13.4 Incident response

If a test compromise is suspected (items leaked, pattern of unusual scores):
1. Affected items immediately withdrawn.
2. Test custodian reviews the incident within 5 working days.
3. If confirmed compromise: affected test-takers may be re-tested at the Academic Office's discretion.
4. Incident logged in the Annual Technical Report.

## 15. Limitations and scope statement

This section is reproduced verbatim on the back of every issued report so that recipients understand what the test claims and does not claim.

1. The PLC-CPT measures **receptive** English language proficiency — Reading, Listening, and Grammar & Vocabulary. It does **not** measure Speaking or Writing.
2. The PLC-CPT is calibrated for placement at Premium Language Centre and is **not** an external certification. CEFR levels reported are PLC's internal placement labels, mapped to CEFR descriptors but not equivalent to scores from Aptis, IELTS, Cambridge, or other external assessments.
3. The test reports a **maximum of C1+**. Students reaching this level are referred for oral interview confirmation. The test does not distinguish C1 from C2.
4. Until pilot calibration and standard-setting are complete, all CEFR labels are **provisional** and may be revised.
5. Placement decisions are made by the assigned teacher in consultation with the PLC-CPT result, not by the test alone.

## Appendix B — Modified Angoff rating sheet

For each item, rater estimates: *"Of 100 test-takers who are just barely at this CEFR level, how many would answer this item correctly?"*

Estimate as a percentage (0–100). Independent estimation, no peer discussion in round 1.

```
Rater: ____________________  Date: ____________  Session: ________

Item ID  | Target CEFR | At A1 level | At A2 level | At B1 level | At B2 level | At C1 level
---------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|------------
GV-0001  | A1          |     %       |             |             |             |
GV-0002  | A2          |     %       |     %       |             |             |
GV-0003  | B1          |     %       |     %       |     %       |             |
...
```

Cut-score for a CEFR boundary = average rater estimate × number of items at that level. The boundary is set so a "minimally competent" test-taker at the higher level would be expected to achieve the cut-score.

## Appendix D — Glossary

- **Adaptive test**: A test that selects each next item based on the test-taker's responses so far, targeting items at the estimated current ability level.
- **Angoff (modified)**: A standard-setting method where expert raters estimate item difficulty for a "minimally competent" examinee at a target level.
- **CAT**: Computerized Adaptive Test.
- **CEFR**: Common European Framework of Reference for Languages.
- **Cut-score**: The minimum raw or scaled score required to be classified at a given CEFR level.
- **DIF**: Differential Item Functioning — when an item performs differently for groups with the same underlying ability, often a fairness flag.
- **EGP / EVP**: English Grammar Profile / English Vocabulary Profile (CEFR-aligned reference resources).
- **IRT**: Item Response Theory.
- **KR-20 / Cronbach's alpha**: Internal-consistency reliability coefficients.
- **Point-biserial correlation**: Correlation between an item score (0/1) and the total test score; an item discrimination index.
- **Rasch (1PL)**: A one-parameter Item Response Theory model where item difficulty is the only item parameter.
- **SEM**: Standard Error of Measurement.
- **Theta**: A test-taker's estimated ability on the IRT logit scale.
